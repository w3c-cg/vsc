This document defines requirements and use cases for representing supply chain events as Verifiable Credentials, enabling interoperable and cryptographically verifiable data exchange across organizational boundaries. It establishes a foundation for mapping [[EPCIS]] event models to decentralized identity-based verification systems using [[DID-CORE]] and [[VC-DATA-MODEL]].

This is a requirements and use cases document, not a full technical specification. It defines the problem space, terminology, and functional requirements that will guide the development of the VSC Core Data Model and related specifications.

This document is a Community Group Draft produced by the Verifiable Supply Chain Community Group. It is not a W3C Standard and may change at any time.

Feedback and contributions are welcome via the GitHub repository.

Introduction

Problem Statement

Global supply chains involve multiple independent actors exchanging critical data across organizational and jurisdictional boundaries. Current systems face three structural challenges:

  • Fragmentation: Proprietary data formats and point-to-point integrations prevent end-to-end visibility.
  • Lack of verifiability: Existing supply chain standards such as [[EPCIS]] define data structures but do not provide native cryptographic verification of who asserted what, when, and under what authority.
  • Regulatory pressure: Regulations such as the U.S. [[DSCSA]] and the EU [[EUDR]] increasingly require auditable, tamper-evident records of product provenance and chain of custody.

The VSC Approach

The Verifiable Supply Chain (VSC) framework addresses these challenges by introducing a model where [=Supply Chain Event=]s are represented as [=Verifiable Credential=]s. This approach:

  • Preserves event semantics: Maintains alignment with [[EPCIS]] event types (ObjectEvent, AggregationEvent, TransformationEvent, etc.) while adding a cryptographic integrity layer.
  • Binds identity to events: Each event is signed by the asserting party using [[DID-CORE]], enabling verification of who made each claim.
  • Enables selective disclosure: Using [[VC-DATA-MODEL]] mechanisms, parties can reveal only the information necessary for a given verification context.

Document Structure

This document is structured as follows:

  • defines key terms used throughout
  • introduces the conceptual model
  • describes motivating scenarios
  • specifies functional and non-functional requirements
  • addresses supply-chain-specific risks

The key words MUST, SHOULD, and MAY are to be interpreted as described in [[RFC2119]] [[RFC8174]].

Scope

In Scope

  • Event-to-credential mapping: A normative mapping from [[EPCIS]] event types to [=Verifiable Credential=] schemas.
  • Identity binding: Specification of how [=Supply Chain Actor=]s are identified using [[DID-CORE]] and how their authority to assert events is established.
  • Verification model: Rules for verifying the integrity, authenticity, and provenance of [=Supply Chain Event=]s represented as [=Verifiable Credential=]s.
  • Selective disclosure: Mechanisms for revealing subsets of event data based on verifier context and authorization.
  • Interoperability considerations: Requirements for ensuring that VSC-conformant implementations can exchange and verify credentials across organizational boundaries.

Out of Scope

  • Consensus systems: This specification does not define or require any specific distributed ledger or blockchain technology.
  • Storage layers: Mechanisms for persisting credentials or event data are implementation-specific.
  • Transport protocols: How credentials are exchanged between parties (e.g., HTTP, messaging) is not defined here.
  • Business workflows: Specific business processes, rules engines, or workflow automation are out of scope.
  • Identity governance: While VSC references [[DID-CORE]], the governance frameworks for issuing and revoking organizational identities are defined externally.

Terminology

Supply Chain Event
A record of something that occurred at a specific point in time within a supply chain, such as the creation, movement, transformation, or observation of goods. In VSC, supply chain events are modeled on [[EPCIS]] event types.
Supply Chain Actor
An organization or individual that participates in a supply chain and is capable of asserting [=Supply Chain Event=]s. Each actor is identified by a [[DID-CORE]] identifier.
Verifiable Credential
A cryptographically verifiable set of claims made by an issuer about a subject, as defined in [[VC-DATA-MODEL]]. In VSC, each [=Supply Chain Event=] is represented as a [=Verifiable Credential=] where the asserting party is the issuer and the event data constitutes the credential subject.
EPCIS Event
A standardized supply chain event defined by [[EPCIS]], including types such as ObjectEvent, AggregationEvent, TransformationEvent, TransactionEvent, and AssociationEvent.
Event-to-Credential Mapping
The normative transformation of an [=EPCIS Event=] into a [=Verifiable Credential=], preserving event semantics, temporal context, and business context.
Verifiable Presentation
A collection of [=Verifiable Credential=]s assembled by a holder for presentation to a verifier, as defined in [[VC-DATA-MODEL]].

Conceptual Architecture

Roles

VSC defines three primary roles, aligned with [[VC-DATA-MODEL]]:

  • Issuer: A [=Supply Chain Actor=] that asserts a [=Supply Chain Event=] and creates the corresponding [=Verifiable Credential=]. The issuer signs the credential using the private key associated with their [[DID-CORE]] identifier.
  • Holder: An entity that receives and may present [=Verifiable Credential=]s. In supply chain contexts, a holder is typically a downstream trading partner or a regulatory body.
  • Verifier: An entity that validates the integrity, authenticity, and provenance of a [=Verifiable Credential=] or [=Verifiable Presentation=]. This includes verifying the issuer's DID, signature, and credential status.

Object Model

The core objects in VSC are:

  • [=Supply Chain Event=]: The actual occurrence being recorded (e.g., a shipment, a quality inspection).
  • [=Verifiable Credential=]: The cryptographically signed representation of the event.
  • Event Schema: The definition of the credential structure for each event type, derived from [[EPCIS]] semantics.
  • [=Verifiable Presentation=]: A collection of event credentials assembled to demonstrate a chain of custody or compliance history.

Information Flow

The VSC model follows this general flow:

  1. A [=Supply Chain Event=] occurs (e.g., goods are shipped).
  2. The asserting [=Supply Chain Actor=] (issuer) creates a [=Verifiable Credential=] conforming to the VSC event schema for that event type, preserving [[EPCIS]] semantics.
  3. The credential is transmitted to the intended recipient (holder), typically the next party in the supply chain.
  4. The holder may present the credential—alone or as part of a [=Verifiable Presentation=]—to a verifier (e.g., a regulator or downstream customer).
  5. The verifier checks the credential's signature, issuer DID, and revocation status before accepting the event as valid.

Use Cases

Raw Material Provenance

Scenario
A manufacturer needs to verify the origin and harvesting conditions of raw materials to comply with [[EUDR]] requirements.
Actors
Raw material supplier (issuer), manufacturer (verifier).
Flow
The supplier issues a [=Verifiable Credential=] for each batch, containing origin coordinates, harvest date, and species information. The manufacturer verifies the credential's integrity, issuer identity, and temporal validity before accepting the shipment.
VSC Relevance
Demonstrates binding of geo-spatial and temporal claims to a verifiable event.

Custody Transfer with Chain of Evidence

Scenario
A pharmaceutical product changes hands from manufacturer to wholesaler to dispenser. Each transfer must be verifiable to satisfy [[DSCSA]] requirements.
Actors
Manufacturer, wholesaler, dispenser (all as issuers of their respective events); regulators (verifiers).
Flow
Each party issues a custody transfer [=Verifiable Credential=] referencing the product identifier and the previous event in the chain. A regulator can request a [=Verifiable Presentation=] containing the full chain of custody.
VSC Relevance
Demonstrates linked event credentials forming an auditable chain of custody.

Automated Compliance Verification

Scenario
A border authority needs to verify that imported goods meet safety and origin requirements before clearing customs.
Actors
Exporter (issuer), customs authority (verifier).
Flow
The exporter presents a [=Verifiable Presentation=] containing inspection certificates, origin credentials, and transport event credentials. The customs authority's automated system verifies each credential against trusted issuer registries.
VSC Relevance
Demonstrates selective presentation of multiple event types for regulatory verification.

Requirements

Functional Requirements

The following requirements are specific to VSC and go beyond general [[VC-DATA-MODEL]] capabilities:

  • REQ-F1: Event-to-Credential Mapping
    VSC-conformant implementations MUST support a normative mapping from each [[EPCIS]] event type (ObjectEvent, AggregationEvent, TransformationEvent, TransactionEvent, AssociationEvent) to a corresponding [=Verifiable Credential=] schema.
  • REQ-F2: Event Semantics Preservation
    The [=Event-to-Credential Mapping=] MUST preserve the temporal context (eventTime, recordTime), business context (bizLocation, bizStep), and product identifiers (EPC or equivalent) of the source [[EPCIS]] event.
  • REQ-F3: Issuer Identification
    Every [=Verifiable Credential=] representing a [=Supply Chain Event=] MUST include an issuer identifier using [[DID-CORE]] syntax.
  • REQ-F4: Event Linking
    Implementations SHOULD support linking [=Supply Chain Event=]s through references in subsequent credentials, enabling reconstruction of event chains for custody transfer and provenance use cases.
  • REQ-F5: Selective Disclosure
    VSC-conformant presentations MUST support selective disclosure of event fields, allowing a holder to reveal only the subset of event data required by a verifier.
  • REQ-F6: Batch-Level Traceability
    Implementations SHOULD support batch-level and lot-level identifiers in event credentials, enabling traceability without requiring per-item granularity.

Non-Functional Requirements

  • REQ-NF1: Scalability
    The credential model MUST be usable at the scale of global supply chains, supporting high-volume event generation without unreasonable computational or storage overhead.
  • REQ-NF2: Privacy Preservation
    VSC SHOULD support privacy-preserving mechanisms including selective disclosure and zero-knowledge proofs where applicable, ensuring that supply chain participants are not required to reveal competitively sensitive data beyond what is necessary for verification.
  • REQ-NF3: Interoperability
    VSC-conformant implementations from different vendors MUST be capable of exchanging and verifying event credentials without prior bilateral agreements.

Security Considerations

In addition to the security considerations defined in [[VC-DATA-MODEL]], VSC implementations must address the following supply-chain-specific risks:

Fraudulent Event Injection

A malicious actor may attempt to inject fabricated [=Supply Chain Event=]s into a supply chain record. VSC implementations MUST verify issuer identity and authority to assert events for the claimed business context. Verification SHOULD include checking the issuer's DID against trusted registries or trust frameworks.

Credential Replay

A [=Verifiable Credential=] valid for one supply chain context may be replayed in another. Implementations SHOULD include context-binding mechanisms such as intended audience or transaction identifiers to prevent cross-context replay attacks.

Broken Custody Chains

A verifier may receive an incomplete chain of event credentials, either due to omission or error. VSC-conformant verifiers SHOULD detect gaps in event chains and signal non-verifiability where chain integrity is broken.

Privacy Considerations

Supply chain data often includes commercially sensitive information. VSC implementations SHOULD:

  • Support selective disclosure mechanisms as described in [[VC-DATA-MODEL]].
  • Avoid requiring globally unique, long-lived correlatable identifiers for all participants in all contexts.
  • Allow the use of pairwise [[DID-CORE]] identifiers where appropriate.

Future Work

This requirements document identifies the following areas for future specification work:

  • VSC Core Data Model: Normative definition of credential schemas for each [[EPCIS]] event type.
  • EPCIS-VC Mapping Specification: Detailed, testable mapping from [[EPCIS]] data structures to [[VC-DATA-MODEL]] representations.
  • VSC Trust Framework: Guidance on establishing verifiable trust relationships among [=Supply Chain Actor=]s.
  • Interoperability Test Suite: Conformance tests for VSC implementations.
